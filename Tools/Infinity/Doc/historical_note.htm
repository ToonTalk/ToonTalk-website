Historical Notes on Cardinality

In the 9th century the star worshipper and mathematician Thabit ibn Qurra, born in 826 in Harran, Mesopotamia (now Turkey), argued that infinity could be split into two, three, or any number of parts, each of which would then have the same size as the whole: there is for example, an infinite number of evens, but also of odds, so each half of infinity is infinity, and so on.

In 1638 Galileo argued that “equal”, “greater” and “less” can’t apply to infinite quantities because a line segment contains an infinity of points, so a longer segment should contain more than that infinity, which is impossible. And again, each natural number has its square matched with it:

Therefore there must be exactly as many square numbers as there are naturals. But at the sametime they should be fewer since the squares grow ever sparser as you go along…

Putting the question aside for a long time seemed to be the best way of coping with it.

The first one who revolutionized our understanding of the infinite was the German mathematician Georg Cantor (1845-1918). He introduced several basic notions: set and cardinality of a set.

To say that these matchings-up show that there are as many of one kind as of the other needs, of course, a very bold leap of thought. We are taking each sort as a completed whole: all the naturals match up perfectly with all the evens (or with all the odds). Stop short and the correspondence breaks down e.g. there are only 50 evens among the first 100 naturals. If you ask: how many is that? we could say: the set N of the natural numbers and the set mN of the multiples of m have the same cardinality, as established by 1-1 correspondence we made. Since we count by means of the natural numbers, we could also say that both sets are countable.

The crucial ideas in mathematic are always so simple as to seem intuitively clear: sets and making 1-1 correspondences between their members. You needn’t even know how to count to do this. In the 4th century a nomadic army from the East rode through the Caucasus into Armenia. “No one could number the vastness of the cavalry contingents” wrote a contemporary chronicler. “So every man was ordered to carry a stone so as to throw it down… so as to form a mound…And wherever they passed, they left such markers at every crossroad along their way.” During some battles the soldiers would set down a stone and those who survived would take each his stone back home.

An 1-1 correspondence between the members of the sets might be hard to find, but once revealed, the two sets between which it ran had to have the same cardinality. Conversely, if somehow you proved that no 1-1 correspondence could exist between the members of two sets, then their cardinalities would have to be different.

Having found that any infinity sequence of the naturals is countable (not a great surprise as it first seemed, if you think about it, since such a sequence will have a first term, then a second, third and so on – and ordering them thus in effect counts them), we could look in the opposite direction – at a set that includes the natural numbers, viz. the set Z of integers (naturals, 0 and negatives whole numbers). What about the rational numbers – isn’t it obvious that they should be more than the integers and thus – forming a more powerful infinity?.. After all between any two fractions will lie another whereas you could always tell the neighbors of any integer. In other words, any set as numerous as the naturals is countable, but how could you possibly count the positive rationals? It look as if you cant’ even start – between 0 and any candidate you name, another will appear… Clearly an order from smaller to larger is not possible because of the ceaseless in-betweens. Or, we could say that “how many” has nothing to do with “how dense”!

What did Cantor achieve? He matched up the members of the set N of the natural numbers with the members of the set Z of the integers, and then – with the members of the set of the rational numbers. Thanks to the notions “set” and “1-1 correspondence” the notion of “infinity” had not many vague meanings but one. The next candidate for a set with a bigger cardinality than the natural numbers was the set R of the real numbers. Cantor managed to show that there was no 1-1 correspondence between the sets N and R by a proof of contradiction. Namely, he assumed that the real numbers in the interval (0,1) are countable, i.e. they could be arranged in a sequence as follows:

0.a11 a12 a13…

0.a21 a22 a23…

0.a31 a32 a33…

…

…

If the assumption that the set of the real numbers in the interval (0,1) is countable, every real number in the interval (0, 1) should be somewhere in this list. Here is how Cantor constructed his great counterexample. The first decimal place in the first number of the above sequence is a11, which is between 0 and 9. Cantor asks us to construct a number as follows. It begins with 0, but the first decimal place would depend on a11 – we put any digit different from a11. On the second decimal palce we put any digit different from a22, and so on – for every n we construct the n-th decimal place of our number to be different from ann. Notice that the number we constructed according to this diagonal method cannot be found on our list – it could not be on the first term of our sequence, since it differs from it at least in its first decimal place; it could not be the second term because at least its second decimal place is different; nor the third, nort the forth, nor the n-th for similar reasons. In fact, it cannot be anywhere on this list which was assumed to contain all the reals in (0, 1) because it differs from each term of the above sequence in at least one decimal place. Thus our assumption that the set of the real numbers in the interval (0,1) is countable lead to a contradiction!

This simple and elegant proof open the gates to what Hilbert called the Cantor Paradise. A set whose cardinality was larger than that of the natural numbers  was found! In other words – there exist infinites of different size!

By such striking insights Cantor faced the challenge of authorities like Aristotle, Gauss and Kronecker who said that it was illegitimate to deal with completed (not potential) infinities. By making infinity actual, Cantor redefined the notion of “number” in terms of sets and so laid the foundations of modern mathematics.
