For Immediate Release

Release of ToonTalk,

a game-like animated world for computer programming.

ToonTalk is a completely new, patented way of constructing, debugging, and running programs.

  • ToonTalk programs are not constructed by typing any text. Nor are they constructed by assembling pictures. They are built by taking actions in an interactive animated world. The programmer is a character in this world and can fly a helicopter, enter houses, train robots, give birds messages to deliver, use animated tools, and much more in the process of constructing, testing, debugging, and running programs. The underlying technology received U.S. Patent 5,517,663.
    •

    ToonTalk includes a game that teaches children computer programming in a new and fun way.

  • Besides including many narrated programming demonstrations and a character who can give users spoken help and advice, ToonTalk includes a new way of learning programming - by playing a game. In the game, players volunteer to help an injured Martian repair his spaceship. To do so they need to solve a sequence of interactive puzzles that gradually introduce programming constructs and techniques. Most of the 50 fourth graders who tested the game enjoyed it and quickly proceeded to build many programs. For example, after an hour or so they had, without any help, constructed a program to build a data structure containing 1, 2, 4, 8, and so on up to 1,073,741,824.
    •

    ToonTalk is a powerful programming language that even 6-year-olds have mastered.

  • In designing a programming language for children, care was taken not to diminish the power and expressiveness of the language due to its concrete and animated syntax. ToonTalk's model of computation is based upon years of research by a group of computer scientists led by the author at Xerox PARC. Children master ToonTalk not by understanding about communication channels and concurrent process synchronization, but by understanding the ToonTalk equivalents: that birds take messages to their nests, and that robots will wait for something to arrive in a nest before doing what they were trained to do.
    •

    ToonTalk was built by Ken Kahn, an MIT computer science Ph.D. with over 20 years of experience in designing programming languages at Xerox PARC and various universities.

  • In 1992, Kahn left Xerox PARC to found Animated Programs, whose mission is to make computer programming child's play. Kahn did all the design and programming himself at his home, and sought out other one or two person home-based companies to provide needed services including animation, voice talent, audio recording, and legal assistance. Besides contracting with a software-testing lab, he has received tremendous help from hundreds of volunteer beta testers for over 3 years.
    •

    ToonTalk is the basis of a 3-year, 4-country, million-dollar European research project.

  • The Playground project began on November 1, 1998. It will iteratively design and evaluate computational playgrounds where children aged 4-8 can play and create their own games using tactile, oral, and physical means of expressing the rules. Playgrounds will be built upon ToonTalk and Logo. The project consists of a consortium of partners from the UK, Portugal, Sweden, and Slovakia, both academic and commercial, with complementary expertise in computer science, educational research and development, and young children's learning. See www.ioe.ac.uk/playground for more information.
